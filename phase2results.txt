Phase 2 Results — Zero-Shot kNN Baseline
=========================================
March 4, 2026


Overview
--------
Phase 2 built and evaluated a zero-shot baseline for the student misconception
classification task. The goal was to establish a performance floor before any
fine-tuning, so we kept the embedding model completely frozen and relied purely
on semantic similarity to the training set to make predictions.

The dataset had 36,696 labeled student responses across 65 unique
Category:Misconception labels. We did an 80/20 stratified split, giving us
roughly 29,357 training examples and 7,339 validation examples. Labels with
fewer than 2 examples were kept entirely in train to avoid empty strata.


Approach
--------
We used the all-MiniLM-L6-v2 sentence transformer (384-dimensional embeddings)
to encode every training and validation example. The model_input column — which
concatenates the question text, the student's multiple choice answer, and their
written explanation — was used as the input string.

At inference, for each validation example we computed cosine similarity between
its embedding and every training embedding, then grouped the similarities by
label and took the mean per label. The top 3 labels by mean similarity were
returned as the prediction. This is essentially a label-level nearest neighbor
retrieval — no fine-tuning, no learned classifier, just frozen embeddings and
dot products.

Embeddings were cached to disk after the first run to avoid recomputing them.
Training embeddings: (29357, 384). Validation embeddings: (7339, 384).


Results
-------
MAP@3 (overall):    0.2004
Rank-1 accuracy:    16.19%  (1188 / 7339 correct)
Top-3 accuracy:     25.30%  (1857 / 7339 have true label in top 3)

A MAP@3 of 0.20 is actually on the higher end of what we expected for a
zero-shot approach — we predicted 0.10–0.25 going in. So the sentence
embeddings are capturing some real signal even without task-specific training.


Per-Label Breakdown
-------------------
The performance is extremely uneven across labels, which tells an interesting
story about the structure of the task.

Best performing labels (MAP@3 = 1.0):
  - False_Misconception:FlipChange          (n=15)
  - False_Misconception:Wrong_Fraction      (n=55)
  - False_Misconception:Wrong_fraction      (n=82)
  - False_Misconception:Tacking             (n=26)
  - False_Misconception:Not_variable        (n=31)
  - False_Misconception:Division            (n=12)
  - False_Misconception:Longer_is_bigger    (n=5)
  - True_Misconception:Firstterm            (n=2)
  - True_Misconception:Incomplete           (n=2)

These are specific, semantically distinctive misconceptions. When a student's
explanation uses language associated with a particular named error (e.g.,
flipping a fraction, adding denominators instead of keeping them), the
embedding model catches it reliably even without fine-tuning.

Worst performing labels:
  - True_Correct:NA           MAP@3 = 0.0000  (n=2961 in val)
  - True_Neither:NA           MAP@3 = 0.0000  (n=1053 in val)
  - False_Neither:NA          MAP@3 = 0.0000  (n=1308 in val)
  - False_Correct:NA          MAP@3 = 0.0000  (n=45 in val)
  - True_Misconception:Duplication   MAP@3 = 0.0000  (n=1 in val)
  - False_Misconception:Irrelevant   MAP@3 = 0.1341  (n=82)


The "NA" Label Problem
-----------------------
This is the most important finding of Phase 2. The four "NA" labels —
True_Correct, True_Neither, False_Neither, and False_Correct — represent cases
where the student either got the question right or gave an answer that doesn't
map to any specific named misconception. Together, these four labels account for
5,367 out of 7,339 validation examples (73.1% of val).

The model predicted exactly 0 of these correctly at any rank. It never put a
True_Correct:NA or True_Neither:NA in its top-3 for any validation example.

Why? Because the kNN baseline retrieves labels based on similarity to named
misconceptions in the training set. The NA labels are harder to identify by
text content alone — a correct answer doesn't consistently sound different from
an incorrect one in terms of surface features. The model gravitates toward
specific misconception labels because those training examples cluster tightly
around particular phrasings, while NA examples are scattered and heterogeneous.

This is a known failure mode of retrieval-based approaches on imbalanced
datasets with catch-all categories. The baseline essentially ignores ~40% of
the validation set (True_Correct:NA alone) because it cannot distinguish
"correct explanation" from other explanations at the embedding level.


Confusion Analysis
------------------
When the model predicts the wrong label at rank-1 (83.8% of val examples),
it most commonly defaults to:
  - True_Misconception:Incomplete    (606 times)
  - False_Misconception:Wrong_Fraction  (499 times)
  - True_Misconception:Additive      (457 times)
  - True_Misconception:Denominator-only_change  (394 times)
  - True_Misconception:WNB           (358 times)

These are all relatively common, well-represented labels in the training set.
The model over-predicts them because their training examples dominate the
similarity space and pull in neighbors that don't actually belong to those
categories.

What This Means for Phase 3
----------------------------
The 0.2004 MAP@3 is our floor. A fine-tuned model needs to meaningfully beat
this to justify the added complexity.

The big opportunity is the NA labels. Any model that learns to recognize
"this student is actually correct" or "this answer doesn't fit a specific
misconception" will gain a lot, since those categories make up 73% of val.
The specific misconception labels (e.g., Wrong_Fraction, Tacking) are already
handled reasonably well by embeddings alone — the baseline saturates those.

Phase 3 should focus on contrastive or classification fine-tuning specifically
designed to separate the NA catch-all categories from the specific
misconception ones, while maintaining the strong performance on the latter.
